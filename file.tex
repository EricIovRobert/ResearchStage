\begin{abstract}
Overcrowding in emergency departments has increased pressure on triage staff and raised interest in using machine learning to assist or automate patient prioritization. Over the past decade, such methods have been applied in many forms, from models trained on structured data such as vital signs and lab results to more recent approaches that also process free-text clinical notes. This report surveys twelve representative papers published between 2017 and 2026, covering their problem formulations, methods, and results, then examines how close the field has come to clinical deployment. Models validated on existing hospital records consistently report strong performance, but validation on new patients in live settings remains rare, no shared benchmark exists, questions of fairness and explainability are largely unsettled, and the regulatory environment adds further obstacles to deployment. The report concludes with conditions a model should satisfy before clinical use and a discussion of research directions most relevant to meeting them, with particular attention to federated learning and interpretable models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}

The emergency department (ED) is the part of a hospital where time pressure is highest and where a wrong decision can have immediate consequences. A patient arrives, and within a few minutes a clinician has to decide how urgently they need care, usually with incomplete information and a queue of other patients waiting. This first decision, triage, shapes everything that follows: who is seen first, which resources are committed, and, in the worst case, who deteriorates while waiting. When the department is overcrowded, which now describes most emergency services in both well-funded and under-resourced health systems, the quality of that decision tends to fall precisely when it matters most~\cite{Dragan}.

Triage works by sorting arriving patients according to how urgently they need care, so that the sickest are seen first when not everyone can be seen at once. In most departments this is done with a standardised five-level acuity scale such as the Emergency Severity Index (ESI), on which a nurse assigns level 1 to a patient who needs immediate life-saving intervention and level 5 to one with very minor needs, judging the level partly from the patient's condition and partly from how many resources the patient is likely to consume~\cite{Ivanov2021}. Other five-level systems, such as the Manchester Triage System and the Canadian Triage and Acuity Scale, follow the same basic logic. Whichever scale is used, the assignment comes down to a nurse combining the presenting complaint, the vital signs and the patient's history into a single judgement under time pressure. That judgement is unavoidably subjective, and studies show that two nurses assessing the same patient will often assign different severity levels, and that a nurse's accuracy tends to fall as workload and fatigue increase~\cite{Ivanov2021}. The visible consequence is a measurable rate of under-triage, in which a seriously ill patient is given too low a priority and may deteriorate while waiting, and over-triage, in which scarce resources go to patients who do not need them. Both are costly, and under-triage can be dangerous.

Machine learning (ML) is well-suited to this problem because every triage encounter already generates the kind of data a model can learn from, including vital signs, the chief complaint written in free text, demographic details and, often, the patient's prior records, all collected as a matter of routine rather than for research. A model trained on enough past encounters can learn the statistical relationship between this information and an outcome of interest, such as the correct acuity level, whether the patient will need admission, or whether they are at risk of deterioration. What a model can offer that human triage cannot reliably provide at scale is consistency, since it applies the same logic to every patient regardless of workload, speed, since a prediction adds no meaningful delay, and a second check that can flag a high-risk patient a rushed assessment might rate too low. In almost all of this work the goal is decision support rather than replacement, with the model offering a second opinion that the nurse remains free to override. A decision that is high-stakes, high-volume, and already generates structured and unstructured data as a by-product is a good fit for this kind of modelling, which is why hospitals, regulators and vendors are now paying attention alongside the research community. The topic also reflects a personal interest in the medical field and, more specifically, in whether AI is capable of making reliable decisions in acute clinical environments and where it falls short.

\subsection{Context of the research}

Computer-based decision support for clinical settings has a longer history than machine learning itself. With advances in artificial intelligence in the 1980s, AI tools were incorporated into decision support systems for healthcare, giving rise to intelligent decision support systems as a distinct area of research~\cite{Fernandes}. Machine learning is one of the technologies that developed within this tradition, allowing systems to learn patterns from data rather than follow rules written by hand~\cite{Fernandes}. Its application to emergency department triage has expanded considerably in recent years~\cite{Sanchez2022}. This growth has produced a body of work large enough that systematic reviews have mapped its scope and assessed its limitations~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. Those reviews show that most studies draw on existing hospital records and that more than half were never tested in a live clinical setting~\cite{Fernandes}. This has raised a set of recurring questions about what it would actually take for any of these systems to be trusted and used in clinical practice~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}.

Can a model trained at one hospital be expected to work at another, given how differently institutions record their data? Do models trained on past clinical decisions inherit the biases embedded in them? How can heterogeneous, partly missing, partly free-text triage data be turned into something a model can consume at all? And, perhaps most important for adoption, can a model explain itself well enough that a clinician will act on its output and a regulator will approve it? These map onto the questions of generalisability, fairness, data quality, and explainability that organise the rest of the report.

Across the surveyed papers, these concerns play out through a range of specific tasks: predicting whether a patient at triage will eventually be admitted~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA}, processing heterogeneous national triage data into a usable form~\cite{Vantu2023}, predicting a patient's eventual disposition as a multi-class problem~\cite{Yoshimura2026}, reading free-text nurse notes to correct ESI assignment~\cite{Ivanov2021}, and combining structured and unstructured data in a single hybrid pipeline~\cite{Wang2023}. The gap running through all of them is that models that perform well on past patient data have so far rarely been tested in live settings, across more than one hospital, on patients outside the training set~\cite{Fernandes, Sanchez2022}.

\subsection{Aim of the report}

The aim of this report is to give a structured and critical overview of the current state of machine learning for emergency department triage, and to use that overview to judge how close the field really is to safe clinical use. The reason for doing the comparison is that the literature is easy to misread. Individual papers report high accuracy, but from those numbers alone a reader cannot tell whether one approach is genuinely better than another, or whether any of them is ready for a real department. The numbers are not directly comparable: studies use different datasets, define their outcomes differently, and follow different evaluation protocols. Beyond that, very few are accompanied by the kind of validation that clinical deployment would require. A critical comparison is therefore needed to separate what the field has actually established from what it has just reported, and that is the gap this report sets out to fill.

In practice the report surveys the main families of methods applied to triage, from structured-data classifiers through hybrid and natural-language approaches to the interpretability and fairness work that now surrounds them. It compares these approaches along the criteria that actually matter: the clinical task they target, the data they use, the performance they report, and the strength of the evidence behind that performance. It also identifies the open problems that separate strong results on past data from systems that could be deployed safely, and draws from the analysis a set of concrete conditions a triage model should satisfy before clinical use. The report is deliberately critical rather than descriptive, because a catalogue of accuracy figures would mislead, and the more useful contribution is to read the literature against the demands of real clinical use.

The rest of the report is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} explains how the surveyed papers were selected and gives a statistical picture of the resulting set, including its spread over time and across subtopics. \section{Methodology of the survey}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section describes how the reviewed papers were found and selected, and gives a short statistical picture of the result. The intention is to make the selection process transparent and reproducible. The procedure used explicit sources, explicit search terms and explicit selection criteria.

\subsection{Selection of bibliographical sources}

The search was carried out through Google Scholar, Web of Science, PubMed, Scopus, and IEEE Xplore, supplemented by following citation trails from the key papers. Together these databases cover the peer-reviewed clinical informatics and biomedical engineering literature, including journals such as \emph{PLOS ONE}, \emph{Heliyon}, \emph{Artificial Intelligence in Medicine}, \emph{Nature Medicine}, and \emph{Computer Methods and Programs in Biomedicine}, as well as conference proceedings in biomedical engineering. Queries combined a term for the clinical setting with a term for the technique, using Boolean operators, for example ``emergency department'' together with ``machine learning'' and ``triage'', ``triage'' together with ``predictive model'' or ``classification'', ``clinical decision support'' together with ``triage'', and ``natural language processing'' together with ``nurse notes''. A further pass paired ``explainability'' or ``fairness'' with ``clinical machine learning'' to capture the more recent strand of work.

Candidate papers were then filtered on four criteria. A paper had to be relevant to the topic, meaning it addressed triage, acuity assignment, admission or disposition prediction, or a directly enabling concern such as data preprocessing, explainability or fairness; general hospital scheduling and unrelated clinical prediction tasks were excluded. Preference was given to peer-reviewed work in recognised venues, so that the survey reflects literature the community itself treats as significant. On timing, papers published between 2017 and 2026 were considered, with preference given to more recent work, while foundational papers from 2017 and 2018 were retained to trace how the field developed. Finally, each retained paper had to be methodologically clear: it had to state its research question, describe its data and method, report evaluation metrics, and discuss its own limitations. A small number of papers were identified from prior work on this topic rather than through the database search, and are included because they directly address the questions under review. Applying these criteria left twelve papers. Several of the included reviews~\cite{Salman2021, Sanchez2022, Fernandes, Antoniadi} themselves aggregate dozens of primary studies, so the effective coverage is considerably wider than twelve.

\section{State of the art in the field}
\label{sec:sota}

This section presents the surveyed work in detail, grouped into the five clusters introduced above. For each paper the discussion covers the problem addressed, the methods used, the main results and the open problems the authors identify, but in continuous prose rather than under fixed headings. Where reported figures appear they are taken from the papers themselves, and where a study does not report a clean headline figure none is invented, since the studies are not directly comparable in any case.

\subsection{The overcrowding problem and the case for decision support}

The starting point for much of this literature is the problem machine learning is meant to solve. Drăgan and colleagues~\cite{Dragan} step back from algorithms to ask why emergency departments become overcrowded in the first place, and what can be done about it, treating overcrowding as a systemic rather than a purely clinical problem. They examine its structural causes and review the technological and workflow countermeasures available, from electronic patient tracking to process-optimisation infrastructure, and conclude that these can move patients through the department faster but cannot, on their own, solve overcrowding. Gains made at the entrance are easily cancelled by bottlenecks further along, most obviously by boarding, where admitted patients stay in the department because no inpatient bed is free. Any machine learning tool introduced to assist triage operates within this same system and will be limited by the same downstream constraints it cannot control. The lesson they draw is organisational rather than algorithmic, and it usefully bounds what any triage model can achieve: even a perfect classifier cannot fix a department whose real constraint is bed capacity.

\subsection{Classical machine learning on structured data}

The most established line of work trains standard classifiers on structured triage and EHR fields. Hong, Haimovich and Taylor~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA} asked whether hospital admission could be predicted at the moment of triage, so that beds and resources could be planned ahead of time. They trained logistic regression, random forests and gradient-boosted trees on records from three emergency departments within one academic health system, drawing on more than half a million visits and, importantly, on each patient's prior medical history rather than triage data alone. Their best model reached an area under the ROC curve (AUC) of about 0.87, and most of that performance came from the historical variables. A consistent finding across the predictor sets they tested was that including prior diagnoses, medications, and procedures improved prediction substantially more than refining the triage data itself, suggesting that for admission prediction the medical history carries more information than the presenting complaint or vital signs alone. The work showed early that admission prediction is tractable for structured-data machine learning. Its main limitation recurs throughout this survey: the study used existing patient records from a single health system, so it does not establish that the model would transfer to hospitals that record their data differently. The authors also left out free-text clinician notes, discarding a source of signal that later hybrid work would try to recover. They also note that their models were built to predict what clinicians historically decided, not necessarily what the correct clinical decision was. A model that replicates historical decision patterns will also replicate any errors or biases embedded in them, and the question of how to move from predicting what a clinician decided to supporting a better decision is identified but not resolved.

Vântu, Vasilescu and Băicoianu~\cite{Vantu2023} focus on a less visible but equally important part of the problem: getting raw triage data into a state where a model can use it. Working with the same publicly available Yale New Haven dataset used by Hong and colleagues, comprising 560,486 encounters and 972 recorded features, they reduce the feature set to 409 by removing irrelevant variables and retaining demographics, past medical history, vital signs, and chief complaints. The missing values are filled, the data scaled, and text fields handled before any model is trained. Their central point is that in this domain the data preparation step can matter as much as which algorithm is chosen, and that results on poorly processed data will understate what a model could achieve. They test three classifiers on the cleaned data: logistic regression, random forest, and a neural network with sequential layers. The neural network performs best across all experiments. The dataset is heavily imbalanced across acuity levels: most encounters fall at ESI levels 2 and 3, while level 1 accounts for only 4,265 of the 560,486 encounters, roughly 0.76 per cent of the total. This imbalance matters because a classifier that simply never predicts level 1 would still be correct on the other 99 per cent of cases, yet would miss every critical patient. The authors run four sets of experiments to explore different strategies for handling this imbalance. In the five-class setting, per-class AUC values for the neural network range from 0.59 for level 1 to 0.78 for level 4, reflecting the difficulty of identifying the most critical and least common category. Applying data balancing improves level 1 detection, raising its AUC to 0.72. Collapsing the five classes into three raises performance further, with the lowest-acuity group reaching an AUC of 0.84. As a further output, the authors develop a prototype web application that accepts patient data and returns an acuity prediction, offering a concrete illustration of how such a model could reach clinical staff. What stays unsolved is the transition from a cleaned static dataset to a live pipeline. Processing a fixed historical export is not the same problem as keeping a model fed with continuously updated records, and the imbalance around rare but critical acuity levels remains difficult to address.

Yoshimura and Moriguchi~\cite{Yoshimura2026} ask a related question: given only what is known at the moment of triage, how accurately can a model predict whether a patient will be discharged or will require admission, intensive care, transfer, or will die in the department? Working on 2,080 encounters from a single emergency facility in Japan, they train logistic regression models on three progressively richer predictor sets. The first contains only information available immediately on arrival, including demographics, symptoms, vital signs, and comorbidities, and reaches an AUC of 0.796. Adding early clinical process indicators such as whether imaging was ordered lifts this to 0.887. Adding laboratory results and diagnostic variables brings it to 0.921. The main contribution is in showing where the largest jump occurs: between triage-only data and early clinical process data, not in the later laboratory layer. At the Youden index threshold, the triage-only model achieves a sensitivity of 0.676 and specificity of 0.784. Adding clinical process indicators brings these to 0.855 and 0.804, and the full model reaches 0.850 sensitivity and 0.855 specificity. The most influential predictors in the triage-only model are age and indicators of acute neurological or mental status change, both of which are available at the moment of arrival without any tests. This suggests that simple bedside observations already carry substantial predictive signal for patient disposition before any diagnostic process begins. The study is restricted to adult patients at one Japanese facility, the cohort is relatively small at 2,080 encounters, and no external validation was carried out, so how the model would perform at hospitals with different workflows or patient populations is unknown. The authors also note that the quality and consistency of triage documentation vary considerably across institutions, which would affect how well the predictor variables transfer to other sites. \subsection{Hybrid and NLP-based approaches}

A second line of work brings in the free-text narrative that the structured models ignore. Wang and colleagues~\cite{Wang2023} work in a different setting from the other papers: referrals from primary into secondary care rather than the emergency department. The problem they address is that general practitioners in the UK send written referral letters when they suspect a patient may have inflammatory arthritis (IA), but only around 40\% of those referrals are confirmed as IA on final diagnosis, and each letter must still be reviewed manually by a specialist before a clinic appointment can be booked. Their framework has three components. The first uses a Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT)-based natural language processing (NLP) model to read the text of referral letters. The second handles structured blood test results with conventional machine learning classifiers. The third combines the predicted probabilities from the first two when both types of data are available; if only one type is present, the corresponding component runs alone. On held-out test data, the combined model reached an AUC of 0.90 in separating IA cases from patients with non-inflammatory conditions. The model also produces a local explanation for each prediction, highlighting the specific words in a referral letter and the blood test values that most influenced the risk score, so that the clinician can check the reasoning rather than simply accept the number. The model was then evaluated in a live pilot at the same hospital in June and July 2022. The accuracy comparison was made on 88 patients referred during November and December 2021, with final coded diagnoses as the benchmark. The model reached a precision of 0.83 and recall of 0.81, against 0.80 and 0.78 for the clinicians. On time, a clinician reviewing referrals without the tool required around ten hours per week; with the decision support that estimate fell to two hours, saving eight hours per week. The main limitation is portability. The model was trained on referral letters from one hospital's rheumatology department, and writing style, abbreviations, and the level of clinical detail in GP letters vary considerably between hospitals. The authors also note that the tool depends on referral data being in machine-readable form, which is not yet consistent across the health system.

Ivanov and colleagues~\cite{Ivanov2021} stay inside the emergency department and target the accuracy of ESI acuity assignment directly. Their system, called KATE, is a clinical decision support tool that applies machine learning and clinical natural language processing to historical records, including the free-text nurse note, and was developed on roughly 166{,}000 encounters from two hospitals. Across all five ESI levels, KATE assigned the correct acuity 75.7\% of the time, against 59.8\% for the triage nurses on the same cases. KATE also matched the expert study clinicians used as the benchmark, who reached 75.3\%. The finding the authors highlight most is the boundary between ESI level 2 and ESI level 3. ESI 2 marks a patient at risk of sudden clinical deterioration; ESI 3 marks a stable patient with complex resource needs. Confusing the two carries direct safety consequences. At that boundary, KATE reached 80\% accuracy against only 41.4\% for the nurses (P~<~0.001). KATE operated without being swayed by the environmental pressures that often push human triage toward error, including crowding, time constraints, and the cognitive biases that arise when a clinician is simultaneously managing a busy department. The authors identify several directions for follow-up work: whether real-time feedback from KATE changes how nurses assign acuity over time, and what effect KATE has on downstream outcomes including mortality, morbidity, emergency department throughput, resource use, and nursing workload. The catch is that KATE is only as good as what is charted. When vital signs or notes are missing or entered incorrectly its input is corrupted and its advantage erodes, which in a hurried department is a real risk.

\subsection{Reviews and systematic surveys}

Three broad reviews cover the field. Salman and colleagues~\cite{Salman2021} provide a taxonomy of how machine learning has been applied across electronic emergency triage and patient-priority systems within the broader field of telemedicine. Their contribution is organisational: they categorise the literature by algorithm type, input data, and clinical goal, with E-triage as one of several application areas. The search covered Science Direct, IEEE Xplore, Web of Science, PubMed, and Medline for papers published from January 2012 onward. A core output is a proposed crossover taxonomy that maps algorithm types to the telemedicine structures they serve, distinguishing between synchronous systems that process patient data in real time and asynchronous systems that store information for later review. The algorithm types represented span supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised, and reinforcement learning approaches, applied to inputs ranging from wearable sensor signals and vital sign monitors to structured clinical records. The review identifies open challenges, including data quality, the time constraints of real-time triage, and the difficulty of integrating heterogeneous data sources, and frames recommendations for future work in both research and clinical settings. A challenge specific to the telemedicine context is processing latency: a system that must return a priority score in seconds while receiving noisy sensor data from a remote location faces a harder engineering problem than one running on clean hospital records after the fact.

Sánchez-Salmerón and colleagues~\cite{Sanchez2022} take a more systematic approach, cataloguing the algorithms and the evaluation practices across a defined set of studies. Following PRISMA guidelines, they searched CINAHL, Cochrane, Cuiden, Medline, and Scopus and identified 11 studies. The geographic spread was mixed: five studies came from the United States, four from Korea, one from Ireland, and one from another healthcare system. Among the models they survey, deep neural networks reach the highest accuracy, followed by XGBoost and random forest, while logistic regression performs worst. Across the literature, machine learning predicts outcomes such as mortality and the need for hospitalisation more accurately than conventional triage scales. The more important observation is methodological: all 11 studies drew on existing hospital records rather than data collected from live clinical settings, and since they come from healthcare systems in different countries with different structures and working practices, the performance figures should be treated with caution and cannot be assumed to carry over to a new setting. The review calls for future work that tests models on data collected after the model is built, across diverse clinical environments, a gap that shapes the critical analysis below. The authors also note that the relatively small number of studies identified reflects how recent this field is, which increases the risk of publication bias and limits the generalisability of the conclusions.

Fernandes and colleagues~\cite{Fernandes} ask why clinical decision support systems for triage, despite years of encouraging results, are still so rarely used in practice. To answer this, they searched six digital libraries including ScienceDirect, IEEE Xplore, and Google Scholar and reviewed systems designed to assist triage decisions in the emergency department. They find that the majority used logistic regression and targeted predictions of hospital admission, intensive care unit (ICU) admission, and length of stay. The most common predictor variables across the reviewed systems were age, sex, vital signs, and chief complaint, and the AUC was the most frequently used performance measure. The barriers they identify are concrete: high implementation costs and the time health professionals need to adapt to new tools. Half of the reviewed studies lacked any clinical validation of the system they built, so the evidence base rests on historical records rather than live deployment. Importantly, in the studies where a system was tested in a real clinical environment, the authors found that decision support did improve health professionals' decision-making and contributed to better patient outcomes. The problem is that most studies never reached that stage. They argue that before the field can claim these systems improve care, it first needs to show that they outperform clinicians in direct comparison and that incorporating them actually changes patient outcomes. \subsection{Explainability, fairness and clinician trust}

The newest strand of work is less concerned with making models more accurate than with making them usable and fair. Antoniadi and colleagues~\cite{Antoniadi} review explainable AI (XAI) in clinical decision support, asking both what has been built and what is missing. They find that systems working with tabular data are the most common target of XAI methods, while systems processing free text are the least common, despite clinical documentation being substantially text-based. Local explanations, which explain a single prediction for one patient, appear more often than global explanations of model behaviour across all patients. There is roughly equal representation of post-hoc methods, where explanation is added after a model has been trained, and ante-hoc methods, where the model is designed to be interpretable from the outset. Post-hoc methods such as SHAP and LIME can open up an otherwise opaque model, but at a cost: they add computation and do not guarantee that the explanation faithfully reflects how the model actually decided. The most significant gap they identify is the near-total absence of user studies asking what kinds of explanations clinicians actually find useful. Without that evidence, it is not established whether any existing method meets the real needs of the people expected to act on the output. The benefits reported in the literature are real: XAI can improve clinician decision confidence, prompt hypotheses about why a prediction was made, and increase the overall trustworthiness of the system. But these benefits depend on the explanation being accurate, and in a clinical setting an explanation that misleads is potentially more dangerous than no explanation at all. Explainability in this context is better understood as an open requirement than a solved feature.

Liu and colleagues~\cite{Liu2025} take on a different but related task: predicting ICU admission for patients arriving at the emergency department. Drawing on 189,167 encounters from the MIMIC-IV database, they compare nine machine learning algorithms across three predictor sets. A model built on ESI scores alone reaches an AUC of 0.68, showing that the existing scale carries limited predictive power on its own. A model using only vital signs reaches AUC 0.76 with Gradient Boosting, and expanding the feature set to include demographics, medical history, and chief complaints brings Gradient Boosting to AUC 0.81. They apply SHAP values to make the model's predictions visible to clinicians, showing which variables most influence each decision. SHAP is used at the level of individual predictions: for each patient, a clinician can see which features pushed the model toward or away from predicting ICU admission, rather than seeing only a summary across the whole population. In the full-feature model, the four best-performing algorithms are Gradient Boosting and Logistic Regression (both AUC 0.81), followed by naive Bayes (0.80) and Random Forest (0.80). The study does not perform external validation, and the data come from a single US hospital spanning 2008 to 2019, so how the approach would generalise to other settings remains untested. The authors also identify variables that were unavailable in the dataset but are clinically relevant to ICU admission decisions, including consciousness status and history of alcohol use. Excluding patients with missing predictor values may introduce selection bias if the missing data were not random, since patients with incomplete records may differ systematically from those with complete data. Addressing these gaps through advanced imputation methods and extending the validation to multiple centres are identified as priorities for future work.

Seyyed-Kalantari and colleagues~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021} provide evidence of a different kind. Studying classifiers trained on chest radiographs, they show that state-of-the-art models systematically underdiagnose under-served groups, with the highest error rates falling on intersectional subpopulations such as Hispanic female patients. The study covers three large public radiology datasets: MIMIC-CXR (371,858 images from 65,079 patients), CheXpert (223,648 images from 64,740 patients), and the NIH ChestX-ray14 dataset (112,120 images from 30,805 patients), as well as a multi-source combination of all three. The subgroups analysed span race and ethnicity, sex, age, and socioeconomic status, with insurance type used as a proxy for the latter. A key methodological observation is that the standard practice in AI-based medical imaging is to train and report performance on the overall patient population without breaking results down by subgroup. Doing so in this study revealed that female patients, patients under 20, Black patients, Hispanic patients, and Medicaid patients all face higher rates of algorithmic underdiagnosis, meaning they are more likely to be falsely classified as healthy when they are not. The mechanism is bias amplification: the training labels come from clinical records that already encode these patterns of underdiagnosis, so the model learns and then reinforces the same bias at scale. The study is about imaging rather than triage, but the mechanism carries over directly. Any model trained on records of past clinical decisions can inherit the biases in them. A triage model built without attention to this risks reproducing a historical disparity automatically and at scale. That is arguably worse than individual human bias, because it is harder to see and easier to trust. The open problem they point to is that subgroup-level performance reporting should become a standard part of model development and post-deployment monitoring rather than an exception. \section{Critical analysis}
\label{sec:critical}

The previous section described what each paper does. This section reads the field as a whole against the requirements of safe clinical use. The surveyed work consistently reports strong predictive performance, yet almost none of it provides the kind of evidence that would justify putting a model into a real emergency department. What follows works through the criteria along which the approaches can be compared, and then through the structural barriers that the headline accuracy figures tend to hide.

\subsection{Comparative criteria}

Comparing triage models is harder than it first appears, because the headline numbers do not measure the same thing. A meaningful comparison has to account for several dimensions at once. The clinical task is the first of them, since admission prediction~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA, Yoshimura2026}, ESI acuity assignment~\cite{Vantu2023, Ivanov2021} and referral triage~\cite{Wang2023} are genuinely different problems with different difficulties and different costs of error. An AUC of 0.87 on a binary admission task is not comparable to accuracy on a five-class acuity task. The data modality is the second, since structured-only models~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA, Vantu2023, Yoshimura2026} are simpler and more portable but discard the clinical narrative, whereas hybrid and NLP models~\cite{Wang2023, Ivanov2021} recover that signal at the cost of fragility to differences in documentation. Reported performance is the third, useful as a sanity check but only within a single study, given that the metric, the dataset and the target all differ across papers. The fourth dimension, and the one that actually matters for deployment, is the strength of the evidence, meaning whether a result rests on cross-validation within a single institution's historical records or on external, multi-site validation carried out on patients seen after the model was built. Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarises a representative subset of the primary studies along these lines, and the pattern it makes visible is that strong metrics and weak evidence tend to go together.

\subsection{Performance versus clinical readiness}

The clearest single finding of this survey is the gap between reported performance and clinical readiness. Models are routinely trained and evaluated on historical data from one institution using cross-validation, a protocol that cannot detect whether the model's patterns hold at a different hospital, on a different patient population, or simply on patients seen after the model was built~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. A model that scores well under cross-validation has shown that it can fit the institution it was trained on, not that it will work anywhere else, and the reviews that aggregate this literature say as much~\cite{Sanchez2022}. My own view is that no triage model should be treated as clinically ready without prospective validation on data collected from patients actively presenting to a real department. External validation across two or more independent sites with different patient populations should also be required. Almost none of the surveyed primary studies clear that bar, the partial exception being the live pilot reported by Wang and colleagues~\cite{Wang2023}, which is also why it stands out.

\subsection{Data quality and standardisation}

Underneath the modelling sits a less visible but equally decisive problem, which is the data. Triage records contain missing values, free text of variable quality and institution-specific coding conventions that prevent a model trained at one institution from being applied directly at another~\cite{Vantu2023, Wang2023}. Vântu and colleagues~\cite{Vantu2023} show how much careful preprocessing and missing-value handling can recover, which is encouraging, but it also shows how much of any reported result depends on data engineering specific to one dataset. Compounding this, there is no shared benchmark dataset for triage machine learning. Every study evaluates on its own data, so cross-study comparison is unreliable and progress is hard to measure objectively. A shared, multi-institution dataset collected over time, capturing raw vitals, chief complaints, free text and downstream outcomes, would do more for the field than another incremental model.

\subsection{Algorithmic bias and fairness}

Because triage datasets are records of past human decisions, they carry the biases of those decisions. Seyyed-Kalantari and colleagues~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021} demonstrate, in medical imaging, that AI can underdiagnose marginalised groups through a mechanism of bias amplification: training labels come from clinical records that already encode those patterns of underdiagnosis, so the model learns and then reinforces the same bias at scale. There is no reason the same mechanism would spare triage. A triage model trained without attention to this can turn a historical disparity into an automated one, applied uniformly and at scale, which is arguably worse than the human bias it replaces because it is harder to see and easier to trust. Auditing model performance across demographic subgroups, both before deployment and at intervals afterwards, should therefore be a standard part of the development process rather than an afterthought, yet very few of the surveyed primary studies do so.

\subsection{Explainability and clinician trust}

A model that cannot explain itself will not be used, and increasingly will not be approved. Clinicians are reasonably unwilling to act on a recommendation they cannot interrogate, especially in a high-stakes setting. Reviews of clinical decision support adoption identify the lack of explainability as a primary barrier~\cite{Antoniadi}. Post-hoc methods such as SHAP and LIME offer partial relief, but Antoniadi and colleagues~\cite{Antoniadi} caution that they add overhead and do not guarantee that the explanation accurately reflects how the model reached its decision, so an explanation can mislead. Liu and colleagues~\cite{Liu2025} apply SHAP to their gradient boosting model to give clinicians feature-level explanations for each prediction, which is a practical step, but applying SHAP in this way remains post-hoc and carries the same limitations Antoniadi and colleagues describe.

\subsection{Legal, social, ethical and professional considerations}

Deployment does not happen in a vacuum. Legally, in the European Union, any system using patient data must comply with the General Data Protection Regulation~\cite{GDPR}, which for large-scale health data processing requires a data protection impact assessment. Medical software meant to influence clinical decisions is likely to fall under the EU Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR} and to be treated as high-risk under the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct}, both of which require formal conformity assessment before use. Socially, the fairness problem discussed above is a direct threat to equitable access to emergency care. Ethically, automated triage raises hard questions of accountability: if a model under-triages a patient who then deteriorates, responsibility has to be assigned among the clinician, the institution and the developer, and the current literature offers no consensus on how to do so. Professionally, clinicians have to be trained to weigh ML recommendations critically and to resist automation bias, the tendency to over-rely on automated recommendations without sufficient scrutiny. These constraints are not separate from the technical work, since they shape what can be built, and engaging with the relevant regulators early in the design cycle is more sensible than treating compliance as a final hurdle.

\subsection{Open problems}

The preceding analysis points to five unresolved problems, listed roughly by urgency. The most pressing is validation. The field has produced many models that perform well on one institution's historical records, but almost none have been tested at a second site, on a different patient population, or on data arriving in real time. Without that evidence, no model can be considered clinically ready, regardless of its training scores. The second is the absence of a shared benchmark dataset. Each group evaluates on its own data, which makes cross-study comparison unreliable and genuine progress hard to distinguish from improvement on one specific dataset that does not carry over elsewhere. Third is fairness. Demographic analysis is rare in the primary literature, yet the evidence that its absence causes real harm is already documented. Fourth is explainability. Post-hoc methods offer partial relief but cannot guarantee that the explanations they produce faithfully reflect the model's actual reasoning; models that are interpretable by design would be preferable. Fifth is robustness: a model trained on clean, complete historical records may behave very differently when it meets the missing values, inconsistent free text and time pressure of a real department. These five problems are connected, and a shared benchmark dataset is the most important lever among them. A shared dataset would make cross-site validation possible, support fairness auditing across patient subgroups, and make robustness testing reproducible. That multiplier effect is why a shared dataset sits second on the list rather than first.

\subsection{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

The analysis above points to six conditions a triage model should satisfy before it can be trusted in clinical use. The most important is validation on live data from multiple independent sites. A model trained and tested on one hospital's records has shown it can fit that institution's data, not that it will work elsewhere. To be considered clinically ready, a model must be tested on data collected from patients actively presenting to a minimum of two hospitals with different patient populations~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. The second is standardisation of data formats across institutions. Without agreed conventions for recording vital signs, chief complaints, and clinical outcomes, comparing results across studies is unreliable and progress is impossible to measure~\cite{Vantu2023, Wang2023}. A shared protocol would allow results from different centres to be placed on equal footing without requiring a single centralised dataset. Third is routine fairness auditing across demographic subgroups, conducted before deployment and at regular intervals afterwards, to avoid carrying forward the biases the training data already contains~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021}. Fourth is explainability. Where possible, models should be designed so that their reasoning is visible from the outset rather than having explanation added after training. When post-hoc methods such as SHAP are used, their limits must be acknowledged: these methods approximate the model's behaviour but do not guarantee that the explanation faithfully reflects how the model actually reached its decision~\cite{Antoniadi}. Fifth, and non-negotiable, is regulatory compliance. The General Data Protection Regulation~\cite{GDPR}, the EU Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR} and the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct} are not optional steps to be addressed at the end of development. A system built without these requirements in mind will need substantial rework before it can be deployed, if it can be deployed at all. Sixth is robustness to real-world data quality. The models in this survey were trained and evaluated on clean, curated datasets. Before clinical use, a model should be tested on data that reflects the actual conditions of a running department: missing values, inconsistent free text, and records entered under time pressure. A model that degrades sharply under these conditions is not ready for deployment regardless of its benchmark performance.

\section{Conclusions and further work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This report has surveyed twelve representative papers on machine learning for emergency department triage. The coverage spans foundational work on overcrowding and admission prediction through to more recent work on disposition prediction, hybrid text-and-data models, and the explainability and fairness of clinical AI. The methods are varied and the reported results are, on their own terms, consistently strong, with admission prediction around an AUC of 0.87~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA}, a hybrid referral-triage model around an AUC of 0.90 with a live pilot~\cite{Wang2023}, and an ESI model assigning acuity more accurately than triage nurses, at roughly 75.7\% against 59.8\%~\cite{Ivanov2021}.

The more important conclusion is not about accuracy but about evidence. Across the field, strong performance on historical training data sits alongside a near-total absence of the external, multi-site validation on live patients that safe deployment would require~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. On top of this are unresolved problems of data quality and standardisation, demonstrated risks of models amplifying the biases already encoded in clinical training labels~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021}, an explainability requirement that post-hoc methods only partly satisfy~\cite{Antoniadi}, and a demanding legal and regulatory environment. The headline numbers describe what these models can do on the data they were trained on, not what they can be trusted to do in a real department.

Several directions for further work follow from this. The most important is a shared, multi-institution benchmark dataset collected over time, with agreed access and privacy rules, which would make reproducible comparison and fairness auditing possible. Federated learning is a promising route to it, since it would allow models to be trained across many hospitals without moving sensitive patient data between them, addressing the generalisability and data-governance problems together. Beyond that, the field should make multi-centre validation on live patient data the expected standard rather than the exception, and apply explainability tools such as SHAP during model development rather than as an afterthought~\cite{Antoniadi}. Fairness auditing should be built into the development process, and regulators should be engaged early under the General Data Protection Regulation~\cite{GDPR}, the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct} and the Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR}. If these conditions are met, machine learning has real potential to improve triage accuracy and ease the load on emergency staff. Until they are, the distance between the literature's promise and clinical reality will remain.
