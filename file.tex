\begin{abstract}
Overcrowding in emergency departments has increased pressure on triage staff and raised interest in using machine learning to assist or automate patient prioritization. Over the past decade, such methods have been applied in many forms, from models trained on structured data such as vital signs and lab results to more recent approaches that also process free-text clinical notes. This report surveys twelve representative papers published between 2017 and 2026, covering their problem formulations, methods, and results, then examines how close the field has come to clinical deployment. Models validated on existing hospital records consistently report strong performance, but validation on new patients in live settings remains rare, no shared benchmark exists, and questions of fairness and explainability are largely unsettled. Regulatory requirements add a further obstacle. The report concludes with conditions a model should satisfy before clinical use and a discussion of research directions most relevant to meeting them, with particular attention to federated learning and interpretable models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}

The emergency department (ED) is the part of a hospital where time pressure is highest and where a wrong decision can have immediate consequences. A patient arrives, and within a few minutes a clinician has to decide how urgently they need care, usually with incomplete information and a queue of other patients waiting. This first decision, triage, shapes everything that follows: who is seen first, which resources are committed, and, in the worst case, who deteriorates while waiting. When the department is overcrowded, which now describes most emergency services in both well-funded and under-resourced health systems, the quality of that decision tends to fall precisely when it matters most~\cite{Dragan}.

Triage works by sorting arriving patients according to how urgently they need care, so that the sickest are seen first when not everyone can be seen at once. In most departments this is done with a standardised five-level acuity scale such as the Emergency Severity Index (ESI), on which a nurse assigns level 1 to a patient who needs immediate life-saving intervention and level 5 to one with very minor needs, judging the level partly from the patient's condition and partly from how many resources the patient is likely to consume~\cite{Ivanov2021, Hong2018PredictingHA}. Other five-level systems, such as the Manchester Triage System and the Canadian Triage and Acuity Scale, follow the same basic logic. Whichever scale is used, the assignment comes down to a nurse combining the presenting complaint, the vital signs and the patient's history into a single judgement under time pressure. That judgement is unavoidably subjective, and studies report meaningful variation between raters and a tendency to drift with fatigue and workload~\cite{Ivanov2021}. The visible consequence is a measurable rate of under-triage, in which a seriously ill patient is given too low a priority and may deteriorate while waiting, and over-triage, in which scarce resources go to patients who do not need them. Both are costly, and under-triage can be dangerous.

Machine learning (ML) is well-suited to this problem because every triage encounter already generates the kind of data a model can learn from, including vital signs, the chief complaint written in free text, demographic details and, often, the patient's prior records, all collected as a matter of routine rather than for research. A model trained on enough past encounters can learn the statistical relationship between this information and an outcome of interest, such as the correct acuity level, whether the patient will need admission, or whether they are at risk of deterioration. What a model can offer that human triage cannot reliably provide at scale is consistency, since it applies the same logic to every patient regardless of workload, speed, since a prediction adds no meaningful delay, and a second check that can flag a high-risk patient a rushed assessment might rate too low. In almost all of this work the goal is decision support rather than replacement, with the model offering a second opinion that the nurse remains free to override. A decision that is high-stakes, high-volume, and already generates structured and unstructured data as a by-product is a good fit for this kind of modelling, which is why hospitals, regulators and vendors are now paying attention alongside the research community. Working out where the field genuinely stands, rather than where its headline accuracy figures suggest it stands, is what this report is for.

\subsection{Context of the research}

Applying computation to triage is not new. Rule-based decision aids predate the modern ML era by decades. What has changed is the move from hand-written rules to data-driven models trained on large electronic health record (EHR) repositories, and more recently the addition of unstructured text and of techniques borrowed from explainable AI. The field has passed through three loosely overlapping phases. First came an early period of showing that ML could match or beat human triage when tested on datasets built from past patient records. Then came a consolidation phase in which review papers began to map the landscape and expose its weaknesses. The current phase is concerned less with raw accuracy than with trust, fairness and the practicalities of deployment.

Several questions come up repeatedly across this body of work. Can a model trained at one hospital be expected to work at another, given how differently institutions record their data? Do models trained on past clinical decisions inherit the biases embedded in them? How can heterogeneous, partly missing, partly free-text triage data be turned into something a model can consume at all? And, perhaps most important for adoption, can a model explain itself well enough that a clinician will act on its output and a regulator will approve it? These map onto the questions of generalisability, fairness, data quality, and explainability that organise the rest of the report.

These questions take specific forms across the surveyed work: predicting whether a patient at triage will eventually be admitted~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA}, processing heterogeneous national triage data into a usable form~\cite{Vantu2023}, predicting a patient's eventual disposition as a multi-class problem~\cite{Yoshimura2026}, reading free-text nurse notes to correct ESI assignment~\cite{Ivanov2021}, and combining structured and unstructured data in a single hybrid pipeline~\cite{Wang2023}. The gap running through all of them is that models which perform well on past patient data have so far rarely been tested where it counts, which is live, in more than one hospital, on patients who were not in the training set~\cite{Fernandes, Sanchez2022}.

\subsection{Aim of the report}

The aim of this report is to give a structured and critical overview of the current state of machine learning for emergency department triage, and to use that overview to judge how close the field really is to safe clinical use. The reason for doing the comparison is that the literature is easy to misread. Individual papers report high accuracy, but from those numbers alone a reader cannot tell whether one approach is genuinely better than another, or whether any of them is ready for a real department. The figures are largely not comparable, because they come from different datasets, different definitions of the target and different evaluation protocols, and very few are accompanied by the kind of validation deployment would require. A critical comparison is therefore needed to separate what the field has actually established from what it has just reported, and that is the gap this report sets out to fill.

In practice the report surveys the main families of methods applied to triage, from structured-data classifiers through hybrid and natural-language approaches to the interpretability and fairness work that now surrounds them, and compares these approaches along the criteria that actually matter: the clinical task they target, the data they use, the performance they report, and the strength of the evidence behind that performance. It also identifies the open problems that separate strong results on past data from systems that could be deployed safely, and draws from the analysis a set of concrete conditions a triage model should satisfy before clinical use. The report is deliberately critical rather than descriptive, because a catalogue of accuracy figures would mislead, and the more useful contribution is to read the literature against the demands of real clinical use.

\subsection{Structure of the report}

The rest of the report is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} explains how the surveyed papers were selected and gives a statistical picture of the resulting set, including its spread over time and across subtopics. Section~\ref{sec:sota} presents the state of the art, grouping the papers into thematic clusters and, for each, describing the problem addressed, the methods used, the main results and the open problems the authors themselves identify. Section~\ref{sec:critical} provides a comparative critical analysis, examining the field against the dimensions that matter for deployment, namely clinical readiness, data quality, fairness, explainability and the surrounding legal and ethical constraints, before adding my own view of the open questions and a set of recommendations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes and outlines directions for further work.

\section{Methodology of the survey}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section describes how the reviewed papers were found and selected, and gives a short statistical picture of the result. The intention is to make the selection reproducible and to be honest about its scope: this is a focused survey of representative work rather than an exhaustive systematic review of the whole field. The procedure used explicit sources, explicit search terms and explicit selection criteria.

\subsection{Selection of bibliographical sources}

The search was carried out mainly through Google Scholar and Web of Science, supplemented by following citation trails from the key papers. These two databases were chosen because together they cover both the peer-reviewed clinical informatics journals, such as \emph{PLOS ONE}, \emph{Heliyon}, \emph{Artificial Intelligence in Medicine} and \emph{Nature Medicine}, and the broader biomedical engineering literature. Queries combined a term for the clinical setting with a term for the technique, using Boolean operators, for example ``emergency department'' together with ``machine learning'' and ``triage'', ``triage'' together with ``predictive model'' or ``classification'', ``clinical decision support'' together with ``triage'', and ``natural language processing'' together with ``nurse notes''. A further pass paired ``explainability'' or ``fairness'' with ``clinical machine learning'' to capture the more recent strand of work.

Candidate papers were then filtered on four criteria. A paper had to be relevant to the topic, meaning it addressed triage, acuity assignment, admission or disposition prediction, or a directly enabling concern such as data preprocessing, explainability or fairness; general hospital scheduling and unrelated clinical prediction tasks were excluded. Preference was given to peer-reviewed work in recognised venues, so that the survey reflects literature the community itself treats as significant. On timing, recent work from 2021 onward was prioritised to capture the current state of the art, while a small number of older, foundational papers from 2017 and 2018 were kept to trace how the field developed. Finally, each retained paper had to be methodologically clear: it had to state its research question, describe its data and method, report evaluation metrics, and discuss its own limitations. Applying these criteria left twelve papers. The number is modest on purpose, since the aim is depth on a representative cross-section rather than breadth. Several of the included reviews~\cite{Salman2021, Sanchez2022, Fernandes, Antoniadi} themselves aggregate dozens of primary studies, so the effective coverage is considerably wider than twelve.

\section{State of the art in the field}
\label{sec:sota}

This section presents the surveyed work in detail, grouped into the five clusters introduced above. For each paper the discussion covers the problem addressed, the methods used, the main results and the open problems the authors identify, but in continuous prose rather than under fixed headings. Where reported figures appear they are taken from the papers themselves, and where a study does not report a clean headline figure none is invented, since the studies are not directly comparable in any case.

\subsection{The overcrowding problem and the case for decision support}

The starting point for much of this literature is the problem machine learning is meant to solve. Drăgan and colleagues~\cite{Dragan} step back from algorithms to ask why emergency departments become overcrowded in the first place, and what can be done about it, treating overcrowding as a systemic rather than a purely clinical problem. They examine its structural causes and review the technological and workflow countermeasures available, from electronic patient tracking to process-optimisation infrastructure, and conclude that these can move patients through the department faster but cannot, on their own, solve overcrowding. Gains made at the entrance are easily cancelled by bottlenecks further along, most obviously by boarding, where admitted patients stay in the department because no inpatient bed is free. The lesson they draw is organisational rather than algorithmic, and it usefully bounds what any triage model can achieve: even a perfect classifier cannot fix a department whose real constraint is bed capacity.

\subsection{Classical machine learning on structured data}

The most established line of work trains standard classifiers on structured triage and EHR fields. Hong, Haimovich and Taylor~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA} asked whether hospital admission could be predicted at the moment of triage, so that beds and resources could be planned ahead of time. They trained logistic regression, random forests and gradient-boosted trees on records from three emergency departments within one academic health system, drawing on more than half a million visits and, importantly, on each patient's prior medical history rather than triage data alone. Their best model reached an area under the ROC curve (AUC) of about 0.87, and most of that performance came from the historical variables. The work showed early that admission prediction is tractable for structured-data machine learning. Its main limitation recurs throughout this survey: the study used existing patient records from a single health system, so it does not establish that the model would transfer to hospitals that record their data differently. The authors also left out free-text clinician notes, discarding a source of signal that later hybrid work would try to recover.

Vântu, Vasilescu and Băicoianu~\cite{Vantu2023} focus on a less visible but equally important part of the problem: getting raw triage data into a state where a model can use it. Working with 560,486 encounters from three Romanian emergency departments collected over three years, they start from 972 recorded features and reduce these to 409 by removing irrelevant variables and retaining demographics, past medical history, vital signs, and chief complaints. The missing values are filled, the data scaled, and text fields handled before any model is trained. Their central point is that in this domain the data preparation step can matter as much as which algorithm is chosen, and that results on poorly processed data will understate what a model could achieve. What stays unsolved is the transition from a cleaned static dataset to a live pipeline. Processing a fixed historical export is not the same problem as keeping a model fed with continuously updated records, and the imbalance around rare but critical acuity levels remains difficult to address.

Yoshimura and Moriguchi~\cite{Yoshimura2026} ask a related question: given only what is known at the moment of triage, how accurately can a model predict whether a patient will be admitted? Working on 2,080 encounters from a single emergency facility in Japan, they train logistic regression models on three progressively richer predictor sets. The first contains only information available immediately on arrival, including demographics, symptoms, vital signs, and comorbidities, and reaches an AUC of 0.796. Adding early clinical process indicators such as whether imaging was ordered lifts this to 0.887. Adding laboratory results and diagnostic variables brings it to 0.921. The main contribution is in showing where the largest jump occurs: between triage-only data and early clinical process data, not in the later laboratory layer. The study is restricted to adult patients at one Japanese facility, the cohort is relatively small at 2,080 encounters, and no external validation was carried out, so how the model would perform at hospitals with different workflows or patient populations is unknown.

\subsection{Hybrid and NLP-based approaches}

A second line of work brings in the free-text narrative that the structured models ignore. Wang and colleagues~\cite{Wang2023} work in a different setting from the other papers: referrals from primary into secondary care rather than the emergency department. Their hybrid framework combines structured referral data with the text of referral letters, using natural language processing to process the written content, and reaches an AUC of 0.90 in separating patients with inflammatory arthritis from those without at the point of triage. Unusually for this literature, the model was used in a live trial at a large UK hospital, where it matched or slightly exceeded clinicians and was estimated to save around eight hours of assessment time per week. The limitation is portability, since documentation styles, abbreviations and local terminology vary enough between institutions that a model trained on one site's letters may not perform as well at another.

Ivanov and colleagues~\cite{Ivanov2021} stay inside the emergency department and target the accuracy of ESI acuity assignment directly. Their system applies machine learning and clinical natural language processing to historical records, including the free-text nurse note, and was developed on roughly 166{,}000 encounters from two hospitals. It assigned the correct ESI level about 75.7\% of the time, against 59.8\% for the triage nurses on the same cases, and it did so without being swayed by the contextual pressures that often push human triage toward error. The catch is that the model is only as good as what is charted. When vital signs or notes are missing or entered incorrectly its input is corrupted and its advantage erodes, which in a hurried department is a real risk.

\subsection{Reviews and systematic surveys}

Three broad reviews cover the field. Salman and colleagues~\cite{Salman2021} provide a taxonomy of how machine learning has been applied across electronic emergency triage and patient-priority systems within the broader field of telemedicine. Their contribution is organisational: they categorise the literature by algorithm type, input data, and clinical goal, with E-triage as one of several application areas. The review identifies open challenges, including data quality, the time constraints of real-time triage, and the difficulty of integrating heterogeneous data sources, and frames recommendations for future work in both research and clinical settings.

Sánchez-Salmerón and colleagues~\cite{Sanchez2022} take a more systematic approach, cataloguing the algorithms and the evaluation practices across a defined set of studies. Among the models they survey, deep neural networks reach the highest accuracy, followed by XGBoost and random forest, while logistic regression performs worst. Across the literature, machine learning predicts outcomes such as mortality and the need for hospitalisation more accurately than conventional triage scales. The more important observation is methodological: all the studies drew on existing hospital records, and since they come from healthcare systems in different countries with different structures and working practices, the performance figures should be treated with caution and cannot be assumed to carry over to a new setting. The review calls for future work that tests models on data collected after the model is built, across diverse clinical environments, a gap that shapes the critical analysis below.

Fernandes and colleagues~\cite{Fernandes} ask why clinical decision support systems for triage, despite years of encouraging results, are still so rarely used in practice. Reviewing these systems, they find that the majority used logistic regression and targeted predictions of hospital admission, intensive care unit admission, and length of stay. The barriers they identify are concrete: high implementation costs and the time health professionals need to adapt to new tools. Half of the reviewed studies lacked any clinical validation of the system they built, so the evidence base rests on historical records rather than live deployment. They argue that before the field can claim these systems improve care, it first needs to show that they outperform clinicians in direct comparison and that incorporating them actually changes patient outcomes.

\subsection{Explainability, fairness and clinician trust}

The newest strand of work is less concerned with making models more accurate than with making them usable and fair. Antoniadi and colleagues~\cite{Antoniadi} review explainable AI in clinical decision support and find that post-hoc explanation methods such as SHAP and LIME can open up an otherwise opaque model, but at a cost. They add computation and, more seriously, do not guarantee that the explanation faithfully reflects how the model actually decided. An explanation that looks convincing but does not faithfully reflect how the model decided can give false confidence. In a clinical setting that is its own hazard, so explainability is better understood as an open requirement than a solved feature.

Liu and colleagues~\cite{Liu2025} take on a different but related task: predicting ICU admission for patients arriving at the emergency department. Drawing on 189,167 encounters from the MIMIC-IV database, they compare nine machine learning algorithms across three predictor sets. A model built on ESI scores alone reaches an AUC of 0.68, showing that the existing scale carries limited predictive power on its own. A model using only vital signs reaches AUC 0.76 with Gradient Boosting, and expanding the feature set to include demographics, medical history, and chief complaints brings Gradient Boosting to AUC 0.81. They apply SHAP values to make the model's predictions visible to clinicians, showing which variables most influence each decision. The study does not perform external validation, and the data come from a single US hospital spanning 2008 to 2019, so how the approach would generalise to other settings remains untested.

Seyyed-Kalantari and colleagues~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021} provide evidence of a different kind. Studying classifiers trained on chest radiographs, they show that state-of-the-art models systematically underdiagnose under-served groups, with the highest error rates falling on intersectional subpopulations such as Hispanic female patients. The mechanism is bias amplification: the training labels come from clinical records that already encode these patterns of underdiagnosis, so the model learns and then reinforces the same bias at scale. The study is about imaging rather than triage, but the mechanism carries over directly. Any model trained on records of past clinical decisions can inherit the biases in them. A triage model built without attention to this risks reproducing a historical disparity automatically and at scale. That is arguably worse than individual human bias, because it is harder to see and easier to trust.

\section{Critical analysis}
\label{sec:critical}

The previous section described what each paper does. This section reads the field as a whole against the requirements of safe clinical use. The organising observation is simple but, I think, decisive. The surveyed work reports strong predictive performance almost everywhere, yet almost none of it provides the kind of evidence that would justify putting a model into a real emergency department. What follows works through the criteria along which the approaches can be compared, and then through the structural barriers that the headline accuracy figures tend to hide.

\subsection{Comparative criteria}

Comparing triage models is harder than it first appears, because the headline numbers do not measure the same thing. A meaningful comparison has to hold several dimensions in view at once. The clinical task is the first of them, since admission prediction~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA, Yoshimura2026}, ESI acuity assignment~\cite{Vantu2023, Ivanov2021} and referral triage~\cite{Wang2023} are genuinely different problems with different difficulties and different costs of error; an AUC of 0.87 on a binary admission task is not comparable to accuracy on a five-class acuity task. The data modality is the second, since structured-only models~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA, Vantu2023, Yoshimura2026} are simpler and more portable but discard the clinical narrative, whereas hybrid and NLP models~\cite{Wang2023, Ivanov2021} recover that signal at the cost of fragility to differences in documentation. Reported performance is the third, useful as a sanity check but only within a single study, given that the metric, the dataset and the target all differ across papers. The fourth dimension, and the one that actually matters for deployment, is the strength of the evidence, meaning whether a result rests on cross-validation within a single institution's historical records or on external, multi-site validation carried out on patients seen after the model was built. \subsection{Performance versus clinical readiness}

The clearest single finding of this survey is the gap between reported performance and clinical readiness. Models are routinely trained and evaluated on historical data from one institution using cross-validation, a protocol that cannot capture the distribution shift that occurs between hospitals, between patient populations or simply over time~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. A model that scores well under cross-validation has shown that it can fit the institution it was trained on, not that it will work anywhere else, and the reviews that aggregate this literature say as much~\cite{Sanchez2022}. My own view is that no triage model should be treated as clinically ready without, at the very least, validation on data collected from patients arriving at a running department and external validation across two or more independent sites with different patient populations. Almost none of the surveyed primary studies clear that bar, the partial exception being the live pilot reported by Wang and colleagues~\cite{Wang2023}, which is also why it stands out.

\subsection{Data quality and standardisation}

Underneath the modelling sits a less glamorous but equally decisive problem, which is the data. Triage records contain missing values, free text of variable quality and institution-specific coding conventions that block direct model transfer~\cite{Vantu2023, Wang2023}. Vântu and colleagues~\cite{Vantu2023} show how much careful preprocessing and imputation can recover, which is encouraging, but it also shows how much of any reported result depends on data engineering specific to one dataset. Compounding this, there is no shared benchmark dataset for triage machine learning. Every study evaluates on its own data, so cross-study comparison is unreliable and progress is hard to measure objectively. A shared, longitudinal, multi-institution dataset that captured raw vitals, chief complaints, free text and downstream outcomes would do more for the field than another incremental model.

\subsection{Algorithmic bias and fairness}

Because triage datasets are records of past human decisions, they carry the biases of those decisions. Seyyed-Kalantari and colleagues~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021} demonstrate, in medical imaging, that AI can underdiagnose marginalised groups through a mechanism of bias amplification: training labels come from clinical records that already encode those patterns of underdiagnosis, so the model learns and then reinforces the same bias at scale. There is no reason the same mechanism would spare triage. A triage model trained without attention to this can turn a historical disparity into an automated one, applied uniformly and at scale, which is arguably worse than the human bias it replaces because it is harder to see and easier to trust. Auditing model performance across demographic subgroups, both before deployment and at intervals afterwards, should therefore be a standard part of the pipeline rather than an afterthought, and very little of the surveyed primary work does it.

\subsection{Explainability and clinician trust}

A model that cannot explain itself will not be used, and increasingly will not be approved. Clinicians are reasonably unwilling to act on a recommendation they cannot interrogate, especially in a high-stakes setting, and reviews of clinical decision support adoption identify the lack of explainability as a primary barrier~\cite{Antoniadi}. Post-hoc methods such as SHAP and LIME offer partial relief, but Antoniadi and colleagues~\cite{Antoniadi} caution that they add overhead and do not guarantee faithfulness to the model, so an explanation can mislead. Liu and colleagues~\cite{Liu2025} apply SHAP to their gradient boosting model to give clinicians variable-level explanations for each prediction, which is a practical step, but this remains post-hoc explanation and carries the same limitations Antoniadi and colleagues describe.

\subsection{Legal, social, ethical and professional considerations}

Deployment does not happen in a vacuum. Legally, in the European Union, any system using patient data must comply with the General Data Protection Regulation~\cite{GDPR}, which for large-scale health data processing requires a data protection impact assessment; medical software meant to influence clinical decisions is likely to fall under the EU Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR} and to be treated as high-risk under the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct}, both of which require formal conformity assessment before use. Socially, the fairness problem discussed above is a direct threat to equitable access to emergency care. Ethically, automated triage raises hard questions of accountability: if a model under-triages a patient who then deteriorates, responsibility has to be assigned among the clinician, the institution and the developer, and the current literature offers no consensus on how. Professionally, clinicians have to be trained to weigh ML recommendations critically and to resist automation bias. These constraints are not separate from the technical work, since they shape what can be built, and engaging with the relevant regulators early in the design cycle is more sensible than treating compliance as a final hurdle.

\subsection{Open problems}

The preceding analysis points to five unresolved problems, listed roughly by urgency. The most pressing is validation. The field has produced many models that perform well on one institution's historical records, but almost none have been tested at a second site, on a different patient population, or on data arriving in real time. Without that evidence, no model can be considered clinically ready, regardless of its training scores. The second is the absence of a shared benchmark dataset. Each group evaluates on its own data, which makes cross-study comparison unreliable and genuine progress hard to distinguish from local optimisation. Third is fairness. Demographic analysis is rare in the primary literature, yet the evidence that its absence causes real harm is already documented. Fourth is explainability. Post-hoc methods offer partial relief but cannot guarantee that the explanations they produce faithfully reflect the model's actual reasoning; models that are interpretable by design would be preferable. Fifth is robustness: a model trained on clean, complete historical records may behave very differently when it meets the missing values, inconsistent free text and time pressure of a real department. These five problems are connected, and the benchmark is the most important lever among them. A shared dataset would make cross-site validation possible, support fairness auditing across patient subgroups, and make robustness testing reproducible. That multiplier effect is why it sits second on the list.

\subsection{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

The analysis above points to five conditions a triage model should satisfy before it can be trusted in clinical use. The most important is validation on live data from multiple independent sites. A model trained and tested on one hospital's records has shown it can fit that institution's data, not that it will work elsewhere. To be considered clinically ready, a model must be tested on data collected from patients actively presenting to a minimum of two hospitals with different patient populations~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. The second is standardisation of data formats across institutions. Without agreed conventions for recording vital signs, chief complaints, and clinical outcomes, comparing results across studies is unreliable and progress is impossible to measure~\cite{Vantu2023, Wang2023}. A shared protocol would allow results from different centres to be placed on equal footing without requiring a single centralised dataset. Third is routine fairness auditing across demographic subgroups, conducted before deployment and at regular intervals afterwards, to avoid carrying forward the biases the training data already contains~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021}. Fourth is explainability. Where possible, models should be designed so that their reasoning is visible from the outset rather than having explanation added after training. When post-hoc methods such as SHAP are used, their limits must be acknowledged: these methods approximate the model's behaviour but do not guarantee that the explanation faithfully reflects how the model actually reached its decision~\cite{Antoniadi}. Fifth, and non-negotiable, is regulatory compliance. The General Data Protection Regulation~\cite{GDPR}, the EU Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR} and the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct} are not optional steps to be addressed at the end of development. A system built without these requirements in mind will need substantial rework before it can be deployed, if it can be deployed at all. These five conditions are not aspirational targets. They are the minimum that separates a model with strong training scores from one that is fit for use in a real emergency department.

\section{Conclusions and further work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This report has surveyed twelve representative papers on machine learning for emergency department triage, from foundational work on overcrowding and admission prediction through to recent work on disposition prediction, hybrid text-and-data models and the explainability and fairness of clinical AI. The methods are varied and the reported results are, on their own terms, consistently strong, with admission prediction around an AUC of 0.87~\cite{Hong2018PredictingHA}, a hybrid referral-triage model around an AUC of 0.90 with a live pilot~\cite{Wang2023}, and an ESI model assigning acuity more accurately than triage nurses, at roughly 75.7\% against 59.8\%~\cite{Ivanov2021}.

The more important conclusion is not about accuracy but about evidence. Across the field, strong performance on historical training data sits alongside a near-total absence of the external, multi-site validation on live patients that safe deployment would require~\cite{Sanchez2022, Fernandes}. On top of this are unresolved problems of data quality and standardisation, demonstrated risks of algorithmic bias against under-represented groups~\cite{SeyyedKalantari2021}, an explainability requirement that post-hoc methods only partly satisfy~\cite{Antoniadi}, and a demanding legal and regulatory environment. The headline numbers describe what these models can do on the data they were trained on, not what they can be trusted to do in a real department.

Several directions for further work follow from this. The most pressing is a shared, longitudinal, multi-institution benchmark dataset under a sound data-governance framework, which would make reproducible comparison and fairness auditing possible for the first time. Federated learning is a promising route to it, since it would allow models to be trained across many hospitals without moving sensitive patient data between them, addressing the generalisability and data-governance problems together. Beyond that, the field should make multi-centre validation on live patient data the expected standard rather than the exception, apply explainability tools such as SHAP during model development rather than as an afterthought~\cite{Liu2025}, build fairness auditing into the development process, and engage regulators early under the EU AI Act~\cite{AIAct} and the Medical Device Regulation~\cite{MDR}. If these conditions are met, machine learning has real potential to improve triage accuracy and ease the load on emergency staff. Until they are, the distance between the literature's promise and clinical reality will remain.
